What is a Theropod?

The word “theropod” comes from Greek meaning “beast-footed”, referring to their three-toed, clawed feet. Theropods are a major subgroup of saurischian (“lizard-hipped”) dinosaurs, and they include everything from early carnivores to modern birds. They are identified by key traits: • Bipedal stance — they walked on two strong hind legs. • Three main weight-bearing toes with sharp claws (“beast feet”). • Hollow bones that made them lighter and more agile. • A mobile, grasping hand (at least early on). • Predatory ancestry, even though some later groups became omnivores or herbivores. Together, these features define the classic theropod body plan—from Herrerasaurus (pictured) to T. rex to today’s birds.

Theropod Groups

Tyrannosauridae

Iconic large-skulled predators with crushing bite forces and highly refined senses. They were the top predators of Late Cretaceous North America and Asia.

Dromaeosaurs

The “raptors,” known for sickle claws, feathers, and high agility—some of the closest non-avian relatives of birds. They’re famous for pack-hunting interpretations and dynamic behavior.

Spinosaurids

The longest known theropod, Spinosaurus was semi-aquatic with a crocodile-like snout and a distinctive sail on its back. Evidence suggests it hunted fish and spent much of its time in rivers and coastal waterways.

Ceratosaurs

Early large theropods with horns and crests on their heads. Dominated ecosystems before the rise of tyrannosaurs. Includes Carnotaurus and Acrocanthosaurus.

Allosaurids

Large active predators with three-fingered hands and powerful tails. Hunted cooperatively in groups. Includes Allosaurus and Giganotosaurus.

Oviraptorosaurs

Small to medium-sized theropods with distinctive crested skulls. Many possessed feathers and likely were omnivorous or specialized feeders.

Troodontids

Small, highly intelligent theropods with enlarged brains. Often feathered and capable of rapid movement. Direct ancestors to modern birds.

Therizinosaurus

Unusual theropods with massive curved claws and pot-bellied bodies. Possibly herbivorous despite their theropod ancestry. Mysterious and specialized hunters or foragers.
