The Top 10 Largest Theropods

Theropods aren’t just famous for their sharp teeth and hunting skills—they also include some of the largest land predators to ever walk the Earth. This page highlights the top 10 largest known theropod dinosaurs, based on current fossil evidence, and gives a quick look at what made each giant unique

Pictured: Deinocheirus and Tarbosaurus — Artist: Clara Takahashi

Largest Known Theropods

1. Tyrannosaurus rex

Size: ~12.4 m | Weight: ~10.5 tons

One of the most powerful predators in Earth’s history, T. rex had a massive skull, bone-crushing bite, and an exceptional sense of smell. It dominated western North America during the Late Cretaceous.

Artist: RJ Palmer

2. Giganotosaurus carolinii

Size: ~13.2 m | Weight: ~8.5 tons

Giganotosaurus was one of the longest carnivorous dinosaurs ever discovered. It lived in what is now Argentina and may have hunted massive sauropods.

Artist: Mark P. Witton

3. Carcharodontosaurus saharicus

Size: ~12.8 m | Weight: ~8.1 tons

Named for its shark-like teeth, this North African predator had long, serrated jaws designed for slicing through enormous prey.

Artist: Vu Boi

4. Mapusaurus roseae

Size: ~12.2 m | Weight: ~7.6 tons

Mapusaurus is closely related to Giganotosaurus and may have hunted cooperatively based on bonebed evidence. Its build suggests a fast, agile predator capable of chasing giant herbivores over long distances.

Artist: Mario Lanzas

5. Spinosaurus aegyptiacus

Size: ~14.5 m | Weight: ~7.5 tons

The longest known theropod, Spinosaurus was semi-aquatic with a crocodile-like snout and a tall sail on its back, likely used for display.

Artist: Heitoresco

6. Sauroniops pachytholus

Size: ~12.2 m | Weight: ~7.3 tons

Known mainly from skull material, Sauroniops was a large North African predator related to Carcharodontosaurus. Its thickened eye crest hints at display structures or head-butting behavior.

Artist: Raul Martin

7. Deinocheirus mirificus

Size: ~11.1 m | Weight: ~7.2 tons

This bizarre giant had a duck-like snout, enormous forelimbs, and a hump-backed silhouette. Despite its size, Deinocheirus wasn’t a predator—it was an omnivore that mixed plants, small animals, and fish into its diet.

Artist: Julio Lacerda

8. Tyrannotitan chubutensis

Size: ~12 m | Weight: ~7.1 tons

A massive South American carcharodontosaurid, Tyrannotitan had thick, robust bones and a powerful bite. It lived millions of years before T. rex but filled a similar apex predator role.

Artist: Path of Titains

9. Acrocanthosaurus atokensis

Size: ~11.5 m | Weight: ~6.3-7 tons

Acrocanthosaurus is known for its tall neural spines, creating a ridge along its back. It hunted large prey in Early Cretaceous North America and left behind trackways showing its impressive stride.

Artist: PaleoPete

10. Oxalaia quilombensis

Size: ~13 m | Weight: ~6.8 tons

Oxalaia was a large Brazilian spinosaur related to Spinosaurus. Its long, slender snout and conical teeth suggest a fish-heavy diet in coastal or river environments.

Artist: Victor Sales
